Proposition: The availability of abortion clinics significantly influences the abortion rate across states.

FOR the Proposition

Design Decisions and Rationale:

  • Black Background: The idea I had with the black background was to create a stark, serious atmosphere in a way that it would be used to make other colors pop, especially contrasting colors like red. My hope was that it would help to elicit an emotional response, given the context (abortion), as it may subconsciously convey a negative connotation. Another thing is that the black background "breaks" the edge of the chart, allowing the trend line to continue following outside of it (-1.5).
  • Blood Splash: The "blood splash" was designed to provoke a visceral/emotional reaction from the viewer. It directly associates the data with blood, which can be interpreted as a deliberate attempt to frame the issue of abortion in a graphic and negative light. The most interesting part is that it adds an editorial slant that is not part of the data itself, and it was placed right at the tip of the arrow to catch the viewer's attention, making it quite deceptive (-2).
  • Sensationalist Title, Caption, and Axes Labels: My goal was to create the most persuasive visualization possible. With that goal in mind, I tried not to open margins for the viewers to think about other possible interpretations of the chart before they even examined it. The title and labels played a crucial role in this aim. I tried to be very sensationalist with the title. I added the words "Escalation" and "Expansion" to the axes labels to "exaggerate" the side of the proposition that this visualization is supporting not taking into account that many data points do not actually show the focused trend (-2).
  • Data Labels: I added the labels only to specific states where we can see the increase of abortion rate together with the increase in the number of clinics to intentionally induce the viewer to pay attention to them. Not surprisingly, the labeled data points are in the quadrant where the viewer also finds the "blood splash," the annotation, and the tip of the trendline (-1).
  • Annotations: I decided to add the annotation to translate the regression coefficients in a more accessible manner to the viewer to give more context to the narrative supported by this visualization that emphasizes the trendline (-1).
  • Bubble Chart Instead of a Scatter Plot: I could also have used a simple scatter plot to present this data, but instead, I used the bubble chart to add more information to the chart and persuade the viewer to think that this is another piece of evidence that shows that the increasing number of clinics is associated with the abortion rate, making the visualization more "crowded" (-0.5).

AGAINST the Proposition

Design Decisions and Rationale:

  • Data, Axes Scale, and Trend Lines: In the upper part of the panel, the bar chart selectively highlights states that have increasing abortion rates despite fewer clinics. The data data was filtered to focus on states with no change or decrease in clinics but an increase in abortion rates. This presentation, however, definitely lacks comprehensive context and only accounts for a partial representation of the data, thereby presenting an incomplete narrative. It is also important to note that we are only presenting data regarding the abortion rate and nothing about the number of available clinics and how this number has changed. In the lower part of the panel, the bubble visualization offers a strikingly different perspective from its counterpart that implies a correlation between clinic availability and abortion rates. Here, the application of a logarithmic scale on both axes effectively compresses variations in the data, masking any trends, particularly because it obscures the states where clinic expansion rates have decreased or remained static. It also excludes the trend line presented in the earlier visualization, further emphasizing the absence of a discernible pattern and suggesting no clear relationship between the number of clinics and abortion rate changes (-2).
  • Sensationalist Title, Caption, and Axes Labels: As in the previous visualization, the panel's title and axes labels were designed to exclusively support one side of the narrative in a wildly exaggerated, persuasive, and sensationalist way. The intentional choice of making the labels of the axes the same as the "FOR the Proposition" visualization allows the viewer to have this second panel as a direct comparative point with the first visualization, possibly making it more intuitive for them to say, "Wait a minute, what is going on here?! I just saw a similar visualization that was claiming..." (-2).
  • Annotations: In the upper part of the panel, the annotation "Nearly 1 in 3 states show higher abortion rates despite the number of clinics" was designed to be straightforward and persuasive to make the viewer focus on the chart's narrative. The word "Nearly" was included to smooth the fact that the 15 states presented actually represent 29% of t She states, not 1/3, and the "blood splash" was used to bring to the visualization an emotional aspect. Besides that, the abortion rate numbers were placed on top of each bar with arrows pointing up to catch the viewer's attention to the "escalation phenomenon". In the lower part of the panel, the annotation "No clear association between clinic expansion rate and abortion rate changes" was added to the chart in a horizontal position right below the data points to emphasize the trend's nonexistence. Moreover, data labels were added only to the same data points labeled on the counterpart chart, possibly making the viewer even more intrigued (-1.5).
  • Background colors: Similar to the counterpart visualization, the upper part of the panel is black to imply a serious/emotional atmosphere. The black again also helped me to contrast the red of the "blood splash" behind the annotation and the color of the bars. In contrast to that, given that the lower part of the panel was designed more exclusively to contrast the visualization that supports the proposition and not show focus on the "dark side" of the narrative, I decided to change the black background back to a soft color (-1.5).

Final Reflection

Reflecting on the creation of both visualizations, it's clear to me now that the line between persuasive and deceptive data visualization is razor-thin. Crafting these two charts was a lesson in the power of subtle visual cues and design choices that can completely alter a narrative. I learned that the same data could be framed in vastly different lights, all depending on the story I wanted to tell. I was surprised by how even minor adjustments to elements like a color scheme or axis labeling could drastically change the impression the data made on an audience. Moreover, this assignment allowed me to constantly draw parallels with the challenges we face nowadays with fake news and misinformation. By being aware of these deceptive techniques in practice will definitely help me to critically evaluate things from now on.

I would define ethics in the visualization context as probably one of the most challenging things. Even though for me it could be summarized as the practice of presenting data in a way that is true to its nature while ensuring the audience can understand it without being misled, it is extremely hard to maintain the integrity of the data without inserting personal bias or allowing design choices to distort the reality it represents. It's not just about not lying with the data; it's about not allowing the data to lie to us.

To distinguish between "acceptable" persuasive choices and "misleading" ones, I believe we must ask if the visualization helps the viewer make an informed decision or leads them toward a predetermined conclusion. For me, acceptable persuasion clarifies complex data, provides the necessary context, and highlights genuine patterns and relationships. However, on the other hand, misleading persuasion obscures, omits, or distorts information to provoke a specific emotional or intellectual response that is not justified by the data itself. If a visualization remains faithful to the data while making it more understandable, it is ethical. However, if the design manipulates the data to serve an agenda, it crosses the line into deception. As a visual communicator, I now see the profound responsibility I hold and the ethical considerations I must adhere to in order to respect both the data and its audience.
